The short version

I build for people. The medium changes.

Some people find one thing and go deep. I went deep on three — and found out they were the same thing.

Whether it's an AI system that erases a week of manual data work, a bassoon part in an opera pit, or a team of developers finding its footing, the job is identical underneath: understand what people actually need, then build the thing that serves them. Not the thing that's easiest to ship. Not the thing that looks most impressive in a demo. The thing that helps someone do better work.

Where that comes from

I studied Computer Science at Princeton University (B.A., cum laude) while earning a certificate in Music Performance — including a stretch abroad at the Royal College of Music in London. Most people treated those as two separate lives. To me they were the same training: listen carefully, respect the fundamentals, practice until the hard parts feel easy, and never forget there's an audience.

Since then I've built custom software used by hundreds of clients, led development teams, and moved into AI-powered automation — work that's less about writing clever code and more about translation: sitting with people who don't speak "software," understanding the process they're stuck in, and turning it into technology that feels obvious in hindsight.

The music never stopped, either. I still perform on bassoon and saxophone, including contracted opera work. It keeps me honest: on stage, there's no backlog, no patch release. You prepare, you listen, and you deliver in real time.
