### The short version

# I build for people. The medium changes.

Some people find one thing and go deep. I went deep on three — and found out they were the same thing.

Whether it's an AI system that erases a week of manual data work, a bassoon part in an opera pit, or a team of developers finding its footing, the job is identical underneath: **understand what people actually need, then build the thing that serves them.** Not the thing that's easiest to ship. Not the thing that looks most impressive in a demo. The thing that helps someone do better work.

### Where that comes from

I studied Computer Science at Princeton University (B.A., cum laude) while earning a certificate in Music Performance — including a stretch abroad at the Royal College of Music in London. Most people treated those as two separate lives. To me they were the same training: listen carefully, respect the fundamentals, practice until the hard parts feel easy, and never forget there's an audience.

Since then I've built custom software used by hundreds of clients, led development teams, and moved into AI-powered automation — work that's less about writing clever code and more about translation: sitting with people who don't speak "software," understanding the process they're stuck in, and turning it into technology that feels obvious in hindsight.

The music never stopped, either. I still perform on bassoon and saxophone, including contracted opera work. It keeps me honest: on stage, there's no backlog, no patch release. You prepare, you listen, and you deliver in real time.

**Meta title:** AI & Development — Taylor Akin, Developer & Technical Lead
**Meta description:** Taylor Akin is a developer and technical lead who turns messy, manual processes into AI-powered software — from custom reporting at scale to LLM-powered data automation.

# Software people actually want to use

I'm Taylor Akin — a developer and technical lead who specializes in the gap most software falls into: **the space between what people say they need and what would actually help them.** My best work starts in a conversation, not an IDE.

### How I work

Every project, the same spine:

1. **Listen first.** I sit with the people who live in the process — the nontechnical users, the frustrated stakeholders — and map what's really happening, not what the ticket says.
2. **Translate.** Fuzzy needs become concrete requirements. Expectations get set early and managed honestly — no surprise gaps at delivery.
3. **Design for the human.** UX that feels intuitive isn't decoration; it's whether the tool gets used at all.
4. **Automate the boring parts.** Increasingly with AI — the goal is always to give people their time back for the work that needs a human.

### Case studies

*(Kept deliberately general — my professional work involves confidential systems and client data.)*

**Custom reporting at scale — from conversation to delivered solution**
For over two years I built custom report solutions on PHP-based frameworks for payroll, time and attendance, deductions, and allocations data. The code was the easy half. The real craft was the full arc: gathering specs from nontechnical clients, managing scope and expectations, designing outputs people could actually read, and executing delivery — **over 100 client onboardings**, plus documentation for complex setups that directly contributed to saving client relationships.

**Building the dev team's tooling — what a team lead actually needs**
As a team lead, I treated the team itself as a system to design. I built dashboards for visibility, an automated ticketing pipeline (Microsoft Forms → Power Automate → Planner), automated quality gates, and unit-testing and root-cause-analysis protocols — the infrastructure that lets a team move faster *and* break less.

**AI-powered data automation — current work**
Today I'm a Technical Lead working on greenfield AI-powered data automation and LLM-powered data migration: systems that take ingestion work that used to demand hours of skilled manual effort and make it fast, repeatable, and reviewable.
